the (weak) Law of Large Numbers

Examples 1 and 2 had random variables with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2.\) The convergence of the \(\overline{X}_n\) to \(\mu\) is explained by…

Theorem: If \(X_1, X_2, X_3,\ldots\) are independent with the same mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2,\) then for all \(\epsilon > 0\): \[\lim_{n\to\infty} {P{\left(\left|\overline{X}_n - \mu\right| > \varepsilon\right)}} = 0.\]

Proof:…

This does not explain example 3 (no variance), and doesn't really explain 4 either.

Good for theory and philosophy. Not so good in practice (rate of convergence?)

convergence in probability

The WLLN is an example of convergence in probability: \[\lim_{n\to\infty} {P{\left(\left|X_n - X\right| > \varepsilon\right)}} = 0,\] and in the WLLN \(X=\mu\) is a constant random variable.

Common notation: \(X_n \overset{P}\longrightarrow X\)

Convergence in probability has to do with values of random variables.

The distribution of \(X\) (what we care about) only deals with things like \(P(a < X < b),\) which is less stringent than convergence in probability.

convergence in distribution

Roughly speaking, the distribution of \(X_n\) converges to the distribution of \(X\) if their cdfs converge: \({F_{\tiny{X_n}}} \to {F_{\tiny{X}}}\)

Formal definition: \(X_n\) converges in distribution to \(X\) if \[\lim_{n\to\infty} {F_{\tiny{X_n}}}(x) = {F_{\tiny{X}}}(x)\] at every point where \({F_{\tiny{X}}}(x)\) is continuous.

Common notations: \[X_n \Longrightarrow X\\ X_n \overset{D}\longrightarrow X\]

Definition not so easy to use.

verifying convergence in distribution

Stated without proof; all imply \(X_n {\overset{D}\longrightarrow}X\).

Theorem: \(X_n\) with pmf \({p_{\tiny{X_n}}}(x)\) that converge to a pmf \({p_{\tiny{X}}}(x)\)…

Example: \(X_n \sim \text{Binomial}\left(n,\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)\) and \(X\sim\text{Poisson}(\lambda)\)

Theorem: \(X_n\) with density \({f_{\tiny{X_n}}}(x)\) that converge to a density \({f_{\tiny{X}}}(x)\)…

Future example for STA261 students: \(X_n \sim t_n\) and \(X\sim N(0,1)\)

The Central Limit Theorem

Theorem: \(X_1,X_2,\ldots\) are i.i.d. with m.g.f. \(m(t)\), mean \(\mu\), and variance \(\sigma^2\). Then: \[\frac{\overline X_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} {\overset{D}\longrightarrow}Z\] where \(Z\sim N(0,1).\)

Proof:…

This is a limit theorem. What is crucial is that the convergence can be fast.

normal approximations

For \(X_1,X_2,\ldots,X_n\) with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) and \(n\) large enough*

\[\sum\limits_{i=1}^n X_i \sim^\text{approx} N(n\mu, n\sigma^2)\\ \overline{X} \sim^\text{approx} N\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\\ \frac{\overline{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim^\text{approx} N(0,1)\]

* depends on the underlying distribution. The more "skewed" or "heavy-tailed", the larger the \(n\) required.
